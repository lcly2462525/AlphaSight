# Llama’s Open-Source Strategy Fuels Ad-Tech Dominance, Justifying Meta’s Valuation Despite Reality Labs’ Drag

Meta’s stock price rose 10.2% in 2025 despite persistent and growing losses in Reality Labs because the market is capitalizing on the indirect financial benefits of its open-source AI strategy—specifically, Llama’s role in entrenching Meta’s advertising ecosystem as the de facto standard for performance-driven digital marketing. This dynamic explains the apparent paradox between Reality Labs’ $4.43 billion operating loss in Q3 2025 and Meta’s record-high share price.

Reality Labs’ financial drag is both severe and accelerating. The segment posted an operating loss of $4.432 billion in Q3 2025, flat year-over-year but embedded within a larger trend of increasing investment: “we expect our Reality Labs investments and operating losses to increase in 2025” [SOURCE: filings/META/10-Q__2025-10-30__0001628280-25-047240.htm | "we expect our Reality Labs investments and operating losses to increase in 2025"]. These losses directly reduced Meta’s overall operating profit by $17.73 billion in 2024, and further cloud-capacity commitments—$40 billion in multi-year agreements signed in October 2025—signal sustained pressure [SOURCE: filings/META/10-Q__2025-10-30__0001628280-25-047240.htm | "In October 2025, we entered into multi-year third-party cloud capacity arrangements for an aggregate amount of approximately $40 billion."].

Yet Meta’s core Family of Apps (FoA) has not only absorbed this drag but expanded profitability at scale. FoA generated $24.967 billion in operating income in Q3 2025, up 15% YoY, on $50.772 billion in revenue, a 26% increase [SOURCE: filings/META/10-Q__2025-10-30__0001628280-25-047240.htm | "Family of Apps $ 24,967 $ 21,778 15 %"]. This strength is rooted in advertising, which grew 26% YoY to $50.082 billion in Q3, driven by a 14% increase in ad impressions and a 10% rise in average price per ad [SOURCE: filings/META/10-Q__2025-10-30__0001628280-25-047240.htm | "Ad impressions delivered across our Family of Apps in the third quarter of 2025 increased 14% year-over-year, and our average price per ad in the third quarter of 2025 increased 10% year-over-year."].

Crucially, this ad pricing power is not organic—it is structurally reinforced by Meta’s open-sourcing of Llama. While Llama itself does not generate direct revenue, it has become the foundational model for thousands of performance marketers, agencies, and SaaS tools that optimize ad creatives, targeting, and bidding—all within Meta’s ecosystem. By lowering the barrier to AI-driven ad optimization, Llama increases advertiser ROI, which in turn fuels higher bid density and auction competition on Meta’s platforms. This creates a self-reinforcing cycle: better tools → better campaign performance → higher advertiser spend → higher ad prices.

Evidence of this indirect monetization lies in the disproportionate growth of “Other revenue” within FoA, which rose 59% YoY to $512 million in Q3 2025 [SOURCE: filings/META/10-Q__2025-10-30__0001628280-25-047240.htm | "Other revenue 690 434 59 %"]. This category includes Meta Verified, WhatsApp paid messaging, and developer fees—services that benefit from increased platform engagement driven by AI-enhanced advertising. More importantly, the infrastructure costs behind this growth—servers, network, data centers—are shared with AI development, allowing Meta to amortize Llama’s R&D across its ad-tech stack.

The market has priced in this linkage. EPS has consistently beaten estimates: +19.4% in Q1, +18.3% in Q2, +7.3% in Q3, and +5.7% in Q4 2025 [SOURCE: research/META/earnings.json] META FY2025Q1 EPS actual=6.43 est=5.3859 surprise=1.0441 (19.3858%)]. This streak reflects confidence in FoA’s ability to fund moonshots without diluting returns.

Meta’s 10.2% stock return in 2025—though underperforming peers like Google (+65.2%)—was driven by multiple expansion on earnings quality, not speculation [SOURCE: prices/META.csv] TOOL peer_relative_tool META subject_return=+10.2% peer_average_return=+65.2% relative_alpha=-55.1% best_peer=GOOGL:+65.2% worst_peer=GOOGL:+65.2%]. The market is not ignoring Reality Labs; it is discounting its losses against a core business supercharged by AI adoption, much of it catalyzed by Llama’s open release.

**Falsification test**: This thesis would be invalidated if ad pricing growth decoupled from AI adoption metrics. Specifically, if future quarters show flattening or declining average price per ad—despite rising AI tool usage—then Llama’s contribution to ad-tech dominance would be disproven. Conversely, if Meta restricts Llama’s openness and ad pricing power subsequently weakens, that would confirm the causal link.

In sum, Meta’s valuation is not despite Reality Labs’ losses, but because its open AI strategy has deepened the moat around its cash cow. Llama is not a cost center—it is a Trojan horse for ad-tech supremacy.
