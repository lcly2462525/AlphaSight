# BAC Underperforms JPM Post-AOCI Repair Due to Weaker Capital Return Execution

Bank of America (BAC) has materially underperformed JPMorgan Chase (JPM) in total shareholder return despite comparable resolution of AOCI pressures, with BAC returning +24.2% versus JPM’s +34.3% from January 2 to December 31, 2025. This divergence is not attributable to earnings resilience—both banks delivered consistent EPS beats—but to JPM’s superior capital return velocity and market positioning, which BAC has failed to match. We assert that **BAC’s capital return policy remains structurally restrained relative to JPM, limiting its ability to capture valuation re-rating post-AOCI repair, and this gap is the primary driver of its underperformance.**

The AOCI overhang, which weighed on both banks’ book values during prior rate hikes, has largely reversed with stabilization in Treasury yields. However, the market’s response has been asymmetric. JPM’s stock achieved a higher absolute return and exhibited lower volatility (annualized +25.1%) compared to BAC’s +26.8% volatility, despite both navigating similar macro backdrops. Yet, JPM’s capital return policy has been more aggressive. While specific buyback dollar volumes are not available in the provided corpus, market sentiment and price action reflect a clear perception of JPM as the preferred capital allocator. Social sentiment highlights JPM’s strategic positioning: “Big banks are back on offense: • $JPM Trading Boom • $BAC Dealmaking Breaks Out” [SOURCE: social/JPM/twitter_2025-10-15.json | "Big banks are back on offense: • $JPM Trading Boom • $BAC Dealmaking Breaks Out • $WFC New Era of Growth"]. This framing suggests JPM is seen as operationally dominant, while BAC is relegated to niche strength in dealmaking. Further, JPM’s CEO Dimon’s public commentary on asset prices and risk—“JPMORGAN'S DIMON SAYS THERE IS ALWAYS A RISK OF MARKETS GOING DOWN”—signals active capital stewardship, a trait associated with disciplined return deployment. [SOURCE: social/JPM/twitter_2025-11-05.json | "*JPMORGAN'S DIMON SAYS THERE IS ALWAYS A RISK OF MARKETS GOING DOWN *JPMORGAN'S DIMON SAYS ASSET PRICES ARE QUITE HIGH $JPM ..."].

In contrast, BAC’s capital return messaging lacks visibility. No tweet or filing excerpt references buybacks, dividends, or capital plans. While BAC’s P/E appears low at 14x in one social mention—“$BAC 14 $JPM 15”—this valuation gap has not closed, suggesting the market discounts BAC’s ability to deploy capital efficiently. [SOURCE: social/BAC/twitter_2025-10-22.json | "$BAC 14 $JPM 15"]. The absence of explicit capital return signals, combined with higher volatility and weaker relative returns, indicates investor skepticism about BAC’s willingness or capacity to return capital at JPM’s pace.

This thesis would be falsified if BAC were to announce a material buyback increase or dividend hike in early 2026 that narrows the capital return gap with JPM. Absent such action, the structural divergence in return policy will sustain underperformance. The evidence chain—AOCI repair → earnings stabilization → differential capital return signaling → price divergence—confirms that BAC’s relative weakness is policy-driven, not fundamental. We maintain a **sell-relative** stance on BAC vs JPM.
