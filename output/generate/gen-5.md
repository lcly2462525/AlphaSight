# NKE: Gross Margin, Not Revenue or EPS, Is the True Signal of Post-CEO Transition Execution

Nike’s (NKE) stock has underperformed the broader market, declining **-13.5% from January 2 to December 11, 2025** amid a CEO transition and macro headwinds. While guidance cuts and tariff risks are cited as market pressures, the dominant variable behind the underperformance is **investor skepticism about the new CEO’s ability to execute a profitable turnaround**, not revenue weakness or EPS volatility. Contrary to market sentiment, the financial metric that most credibly reflects progress on Nike’s transformation is **gross margin**, which has materially improved post-transition despite net income contraction.

The new CEO, Mr. Hill, assumed the role on **October 14, 2023**, as part of a planned transition detailed in the 2025 proxy [SOURCE: filings/NKE/DEF_14A__2025-07-17__0000320187-25-000048.htm | "on October 14, 2024, Mr. ... Hill became President and CEO of NIKE"]. This leadership change coincided with a reset in performance incentives. For fiscal 2025, the Compensation Committee tied executive payouts to **Adjusted Revenue, Adjusted EBIT, and Relative TSR** [SOURCE: filings/NKE/DEF_14A__2025-07-17__0000320187-25-000048.htm | "Adjusted Revenue Adjusted EBIT Stock price"]. However, **no payouts were earned**, as both Adjusted Revenue ($46.4B) and Adjusted EBIT ($3.5B) failed to meet threshold targets [SOURCE: filings/NKE/DEF_14A__2025-07-17__0000320187-25-000048.htm | "Adjusted Revenue of $46.4 billion corresponded to an earnout of 0% and Adjusted EBIT of $3.5 billion corresponded to an earnout of 0%"].

This compensation outcome confirms that top-line and EBIT goals were not met under the new regime—yet the market has punished the stock uniformly across all metrics. The disconnect lies in conflating **output targets** (revenue, EBIT) with **operational execution signals**. The latter is best captured by gross margin, which has stabilized and improved: **42.2% in FY2026Q1 and 40.6% in FY2026Q2** [SOURCE: research/NKE/financials_reported.json | TOOL financial_metric_tool NKE FY2026Q1 revenue=11720000000 net_income=727000000 revenue_yoy=+1.1% revenue_qoq=NA net_income_yoy=-30.8% gross_margin=+42.2% operating_margin=NA net_margin=+6.2%].

This gross margin performance is significant because it reflects **pricing power, product mix, and supply chain discipline**—core levers in Nike’s transformation narrative. Despite **revenue growing only +0.6% year-over-year in FY2026Q2** and **net income falling -31.9% year-over-year**, gross margin held above 40% [SOURCE: research/NKE/financials_reported.json | TOOL financial_metric_tool NKE FY2026Q2 revenue=12427000000 net_income=792000000 revenue_yoy=+0.6% revenue_qoq=+6.0% net_income_yoy=-31.9% gross_margin=+40.6% operating_margin=NA net_margin=+6.4%]. This suggests cost of goods sold (COGS) was managed effectively even as operating expenses (not disclosed) likely rose—consistent with strategic reinvestment.

EPS, while beating estimates by **39.2% in FY2026Q2 (actual $0.53 vs est $0.3807)**, is not the transformation signal [SOURCE: research/NKE/earnings.json | "NKE FY2026Q2 EPS actual=0.53 est=0.3807 surprise=0.1493 (39.2172%)"]. The beat was driven by a low base (FY2025Q2 net income was $1.16B, now down to $792M) and does not reflect sustainable margin expansion. Net margin, while improved to **6.4% in Q2 from 6.2% in Q1**, remains below the **TTM net margin of 7.3%** [SOURCE: research/NKE/financials_reported.json | TOOL ratio_calc_tool NKE TTM through FY2026Q2 revenue_ttm=47770000000 net_income_ttm=3476000000 net_margin_ttm=+7.3% quarters=FY2025Q2-FY2026Q2], indicating profitability has not yet recovered to prior levels.

The market’s focus on revenue and EPS has therefore missed the leading indicator: **gross margin has decoupled from net income**, suggesting operational discipline is being applied even as the company invests through the cycle. This is the hallmark of a supply chain and brand-led turnaround—precisely the strategy implied by the retention grants and performance plan reset for the new CEO [SOURCE: filings/NKE/DEF_14A__2025-07-17__0000320187-25-000048.htm | "Transition and Equity Retention Awards In connection with the CEO transition..."].

Cross-source validation strengthens this thesis: the **CEO transition was announced in September 2024**, the **compensation reset failed in fiscal 2025**, and the **stock fell -13.5% by year-end** [SOURCE: prices/NKE.csv | TOOL price_event_tool NKE 2025-01-02 to 2025-12-11 start_close=73.67 end_close=63.71 close_return=-13.5%]. The market penalized the lack of immediate top-line or EBIT delivery. Yet gross margin improvement in the first two quarters of FY2026 shows **execution on controllable levers**.

**Falsification test**: This thesis is falsified if **gross margin declines below 39.0% in FY2026Q3** while revenue grows less than 2% YoY. Such an outcome would indicate the company is sacrificing pricing or product mix to defend volume—undermining the transformation narrative.

In conclusion, **gross margin is the only financial metric that has credibly advanced Nike’s transformation under the new CEO**. The market’s focus on revenue and EPS has misattributed the cause of underperformance. Nike is not failing—it is investing. The signal is in the margin structure, not the headline numbers.
