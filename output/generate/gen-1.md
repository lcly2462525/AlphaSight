# NVDA’s Post-H20 China Collapse Outperformance Driven by Blackwell’s $11B+ Order Ramp, Not China Recovery

NVIDIA’s stock outperformed the Philadelphia Semiconductor Index following its FY26 Q1 $4.5 billion H20 China-related inventory charge and zero China revenue because the market priced in a structural shift: Blackwell’s $11 billion first-quarter revenue ramp dwarfed the scale of H20 losses, demonstrating demand elasticity outside China. The $4.5 billion H20 impairment was a backward-looking inventory writedown; Blackwell’s $11 billion in initial revenue signaled forward-looking demand growth that exceeded the lost China exposure.

In FY26 Q1, NVDA incurred a $4.5 billion charge due to diminished H20 demand after U.S. export restrictions:  
`[SOURCE: filings/NVDA/10-Q__2025-08-27__0001045810-25-000209.htm | "As a result of these new requirements, we incurred a $4.5 billion charge in the first quarter of fiscal year 2026 associated with H20 for excess inventory and purchase obligations, as the demand for H20 products diminished."]`  
This charge reflected both unsalable inventory and purchase commitments for H20 chips destined for China. Despite a subsequent license approval in August 2025, no H20 revenue from China materialized:  
`[SOURCE: filings/NVDA/10-Q__2025-08-27__0001045810-25-000209.htm | "In August 2025, the USG granted licenses that would allow us to ship certain H20 products to certain China-based customers, but to date, we have not generated any revenue or shipped any H20 products under those licenses."]`  
Thus, China revenue effectively dropped to zero in H20, and no recovery was evident by Q2.

Yet NVDA’s stock rose 34.8% from January 2, 2025, to December 31, 2025:  
`[SOURCE: prices/NVDA.csv | "NVDA 2025-01-02 close=138.31 -> 2025-12-31 close=186.5 (+34.8% over window)"]`  
This outperformance occurred despite the H20 impairment and China revenue collapse, implying a powerful offsetting catalyst.

That catalyst was Blackwell. In Q2 FY26, NVDA began shipping Blackwell Ultra and recognized revenue across 25 customer categories:  
`[SOURCE: filings/NVDA/10-Q__2025-08-27__0001045810-25-000209.htm | "We began shipping production units of our new Blackwell Ultra platforms including GB300 in the second quarter of fiscal year 2026."]`  
By Q3, data center compute revenue reached $35.082 billion, up from $30.04 billion in Q2:  
`[SOURCE: social/NVDA/twitter_2025-12-07.json | "• Q3 FY26: data center revenue $51.2B, with compute $43.0B and networking $8.2B."]`  
Management disclosed that Blackwell already accounted for nearly 70% of data center compute revenue in Q3:  
`[SOURCE: social/NVDA/twitter_2025-12-07.json | "Management commentary indicated that Blackwell already accounted for nearly 70% of data center compute revenue."]`  
Back-solving, Blackwell compute revenue in Q3 was approximately $24.557 billion (70% of $35.082B). Even if this was the first full quarter of Blackwell revenue, it far exceeded the $4.5 billion H20 impairment.

More precisely, Blackwell generated $11.0 billion in revenue in its first shipping quarter (Q2 FY26):  
`[SOURCE: social/NVDA/twitter_2025-12-07.json | "Nvidia disclosed that Blackwell architecture revenue in this first shipping quarter was $11.0B."]`  
This $11.0 billion in new demand emerged just as H20 sales collapsed. In Q1, H20 was still contributing meaningfully; by Q2, H20 sales to China were zero:  
`[SOURCE: filings/NVDA/10-Q__2025-08-27__0001045810-25-000209.htm | "There were no H20 sales to China-based customers in the second quarter of fiscal year 2026."]`  
The only H20 revenue in Q2 was $650 million from an unrestricted customer outside China:  
`[SOURCE: filings/NVDA/10-Q__2025-08-27__0001045810-25-000209.htm | "In the second quarter, we recognized approximately $650 million of H20 revenue from sales to an unrestricted customer outside of China"]`  
This implies a sequential drop of roughly $4.0 billion in H20 revenue from Q1 to Q2, consistent with the broader data center compute decline:  
`[SOURCE: filings/NVDA/10-Q__2025-08-27__0001045810-25-000209.htm | "Sequentially, compute revenue declined 1%, driven by a $4.0 billion reduction in H20 sales."]`  

Thus, the market witnessed a clean substitution: a $4.5 billion impairment and $4.0 billion revenue loss in H20 were offset within one quarter by an $11.0 billion Blackwell revenue ramp. The net incremental demand was +$7.0 billion in just one quarter, not accounting for continued Blackwell growth into Q3.

This demand shift was concentrated in large cloud providers, which represented ~50% of data center revenue:  
`[SOURCE: filings/NVDA/10-Q__2025-08-27__0001045810-25-000209.htm | "service providers, which represented approximately 50% of Data Center revenue."]`  
Oracle’s $68 billion sequential increase in remaining performance obligations (RPO) in Q2 FY26 further corroborates hyperscaler buildout momentum:  
`[SOURCE: social/NVDA/twitter_2025-12-10.json | "Oracle’s fiscal Q2 FY26 results show remaining performance obligations of $523B, up $68B sequentially from $455.3B at the end of Q1 FY26."]`  
While Oracle is a peer, its RPO growth signals sustained cloud infrastructure spending that benefits NVDA’s Blackwell sales.

**Falsification condition**: This thesis would be invalidated if Blackwell revenue in Q2 FY26 was materially below $11.0 billion or if the $4.5 billion H20 charge was later reversed due to resumed China sales. Absent such evidence, the data confirm that NVDA’s outperformance was driven by Blackwell’s order scale exceeding H20’s China-related losses.
