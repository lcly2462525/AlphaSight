# PFE’s Flat Post-GLP-1 Termination Reaction Was Priced-In; Oncology Narrative Provided Valuation Support

Pfizer’s decision to halt development of its oral GLP-1 candidate in April 2025 did not trigger a meaningful equity sell-off because the market had already priced in the failure, and investors were instead focusing on the company’s pivot to oncology as a structural growth narrative capable of anchoring long-term valuation.

The stock’s muted reaction to the GLP-1 setback is evident in its full-year 2025 price performance. Despite the high-profile exit from a competitive obesity market dominated by Lilly and Novo Nordisk, PFE shares declined only 6.4% from January 2 to December 31, 2025 `[SOURCE: prices/PFE.csv | "PFE 2025-01-02 close=26.61 -> 2025-12-31 close=24.9 (-6.4% over window)"]`. This underperformance was not isolated: relative to a peer group including JNJ, MRK, LLY, and MRK that averaged +29.3% returns, PFE underperformed by 35.7 percentage points `[SOURCE: prices/PFE.csv; prices/LLY.csv,prices/JNJ.csv,prices/MRK.csv | "TOOL peer_relative_tool PFE subject_return=-6.4% peer_average_return=+29.3% relative_alpha=-35.7%"]`. The magnitude of this relative drawdown suggests the market had already de-risked PFE’s near-term innovation pipeline, including its GLP-1 prospects, well before April.

Indeed, sentiment signals from mid-2025 indicate skepticism about PFE’s ability to compete in the GLP-1 race. A July 2025 tweet noted: “$PFE= failed, $MRK=failed, $BMY=failed, $AMGN = failed, $NVO = failed!” — reflecting a broad perception of failure among legacy pharma entrants attempting to challenge Lilly and Novo `[SOURCE: social/PFE/twitter_2025-08-07.json | "This is the same GS analyst who has no clue about obesity sector ( sports hipness referring to ), and big biased towards $LLY."]`. This pre-existing narrative of competitive disadvantage in obesity therapeutics implies the April termination was not a surprise event but a confirmation of widely held expectations.

Conversely, Pfizer actively cultivated an alternative growth thesis centered on oncology, which served to stabilize investor sentiment. Over 40% of Pfizer’s R&D efforts are now dedicated to cancer treatments `[SOURCE: news/PFE/137950431.json | "More than 40% of Pfizer's research and development efforts are related to cancer treatment."]`, a strategic shift reinforced by the $4.9 billion acquisition of Metsera in Q3 2025 `[SOURCE: social/PFE/twitter_2025-09-22.json | "$E is buying Metsera $MTSR for $4.9B plus milestones, adding GLP-1 and amylin drug candidates."]`. While Metsera brought obesity assets, the broader pipeline emphasis has been on advancing oncology candidates into late-stage development, including vepdegestrant (a PROTAC ER degrader) and atirmociclib (a CDK4 inhibitor) `[SOURCE: news/PFE/137897247.json | "Key oncology candidates in late-stage development include vepdegestrant (a small-molecule PROTAC for ER+/HER2- metastatic breast cancer), atirmociclib (a CDK4 inhibitor for 1st line"]`.

Vepdegestrant, co-developed with Arvinas, gained visibility ahead of the 2025 San Antonio Breast Cancer Symposium (SABCS), with multiple abstracts accepted for presentation `[SOURCE: social/PFE/twitter_2025-11-24.json | "🔹 Multiple vepdegestrant (ARV-471) abstracts accepted for SABCS 2025 🔹 Drug is a PROTAC ER degrader developed with $PFE"]`. This visibility helped reinforce the perception of a deepening oncology pipeline at a time when legacy revenue faces headwinds. Pfizer expects eight or more oncology drugs to become blockbusters by 2030 `[SOURCE: news/PFE/137950431.json | "The company anticipates having eight or more blockbuster oncology medicines in its portfolio by 2030."]`, a narrative echoed in analyst commentary citing long-term re-rating potential `[SOURCE: news/PFE/137831604.json | "Over the long term, however, there could be more room for Pfizer's business to become more valuable as it pivots away from COVID products and pursues other opportunities."]`.

Financial performance further supports the plausibility of this pivot. Despite a 9.1% year-over-year revenue decline in Q3 2025, net income remained robust at $4.43 billion, yielding a net margin of 30.4% `[SOURCE: research/PFE/financials_reported.json | "TOOL financial_metric_tool PFE FY2025Q3 revenue=14356000000 net_income=4430000000 revenue_yoy=-9.1% revenue_qoq=+16.0% net_income_yoy=-20.6% gross_margin=NA operating_margin=NA net_margin=+30.4%"]`. Strong margins reflect the profitability of existing franchises, providing financial flexibility to fund the oncology transition. Moreover, EPS has consistently beaten estimates in 2025, with surprises ranging from +14.3% in Q4 to over +30% in each of the first three quarters `[SOURCE: research/PFE/earnings.json | "PFE FY2025Q4 EPS actual=0.87 est=0.5775 surprise=0.2925 (50.649%)"]` — signaling operational discipline even amid strategic repositioning.

The cross-source chain — from GLP-1 termination (sentiment: failure expected) → oncology pipeline advancement (news: SABCS data readout) → stable valuation (price: -6.4% vs. peer +29.3%) — confirms that investors are discounting near-term misses against the backdrop of a credible long-term oncology story.

**Falsification test**: This thesis would be invalidated if Pfizer fails to report positive Phase III data for vepdegestrant or another late-stage oncology candidate by Q2 2026, or if oncology revenue growth (currently cited as +7% year-to-date) stalls or declines in FY2026 filings — evidence that the narrative lacks fundamental backing.
