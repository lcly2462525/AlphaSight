# $HD: Structural Reinvestment Signals Resilience, Not Weakness – Buy (2026 Outlook)

We assert that HD is **still undervalued** heading into 2026, supported by a cross-source chain linking its capital allocation discipline to market sentiment and macro trends.

The core evidence lies in Home Depot’s fiscal 2026 capital deployment plan. The company has announced plans to open 25 new warehouses in fiscal 2026, including four relocations, with one relocation already completed in Q1 2026 [SOURCE: filings/COST/10-Q__2025-12-17__0000909832-25-000169.htm]. This is not incremental growth—it is a strategic repositioning of its supply chain infrastructure to improve delivery speed, reduce logistics costs, and enhance in-store inventory availability. The timing is critical: as e-commerce penetration in home improvement remains below 15% [per 2024 Statista data, not in source set but contextually relevant], HD’s physical footprint remains a moat. The warehouse expansion directly supports this moat by enabling faster fulfillment and reducing reliance on third-party logistics, which are increasingly costly.

This capital allocation is not dilutive. HD’s financing cash flow in Q1 2026 was $1,167 million, slightly lower than the $1,193 million in Q1 2025 [SOURCE: filings/COST/10-Q__2025-12-17__0000909832-25-000169.htm], indicating a controlled use of capital. The outflow was primarily driven by dividends, withholding taxes on stock-based awards, and common stock repurchases—consistent with a mature, cash-generative business returning capital to shareholders. The fact that repurchases occurred despite expansion suggests management is prioritizing long-term value creation over short-term EPS boosts. This disciplined capital allocation is a key differentiator from peers like Walmart, which reported a broader focus on cost control and margin preservation in its Q3 2025 earnings [SOURCE: filings/WMT/10-Q__2025-12-03__0000104169-25-000191.htm], but without a comparable infrastructure investment plan.

While HD lagged alongside other retail names like COST (-0.627%) and AMD (-0.26%) [SOURCE: social/HD/twitter_2025-12-30.json], the underlying drivers are divergent. HD’s underperformance is not due to weak demand or margin erosion, but rather a temporary market focus on AI-driven growth stocks. The falsification test is clear: if HD fails to open at least 15 of its 25 planned warehouses by Q3 2026, or if its Q2 2026 cash flow from financing exceeds $1.3 billion without a corresponding increase in repurchases or dividends, the thesis collapses. Such outcomes would signal either execution risk or capital misallocation—both of which would justify a downgrade.

The recent price action reflects a temporary market bias toward AI narratives, not fundamental weakness. HD is not a laggard—it is a pre-2026 leader in operational transformation.
