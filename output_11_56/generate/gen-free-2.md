# NVDA Is Priced for Perfection: Earnings Momentum Masks Escalating Inventory Risk

NVIDIA (NVDA) is **priced for perfection**, and its current valuation does not adequately reflect the escalating risk from inventory accumulation and capital intensity, despite continued earnings outperformance. While revenue and EPS growth remain strong, the surge in inventory provisions and unpaid property/equipment commitments signals growing operational strain that could pressure margins and cash flow in FY2026H2. This risk is not yet priced in, as the stock rose 34.8% in 2025 despite deteriorating cost controls.

NVDA’s FY2026Q3 revenue reached $147.8 billion with net income of $77.1 billion, up sharply from $44.1 billion revenue and $18.8 billion net income in Q1 [SOURCE: research/NVDA/financials_reported.json | "NVDA FY2026Q3 revenue=147811000000.0 net_income=77107000000.0"]. EPS has consistently beat estimates: $1.30 actual vs. $1.2746 expected in Q3, $1.05 vs. $1.0281 in Q2, and $0.96 vs. $0.9521 in Q1 [SOURCE: research/NVDA/earnings.json | "NVDA FY2026Q3 EPS actual=1.3 est=1.2746 surprise=0.0254 (1.9928%)"]. These beats have fueled investor confidence and a 34.8% stock price increase from $138.31 on January 2, 2025, to $186.50 on December 31, 2025 [SOURCE: prices/NVDA.csv | "NVDA 2025-01-02 close=138.31 -> 2025-12-31 close=186.5 (+34.8% over window)"].

However, this growth is being underwritten by aggressive inventory and capital spending. NVDA recorded $378 million in inventory provisions in FY2026Q3 alone, up from $322 million in the same quarter a year prior [SOURCE: filings/NVDA/10-Q__2025-11-19__0001045810-25-000230.htm | "We recorded inventory provisions of $ 378 million and $ 322 million for the third quarter of fiscal years 2026 and 2025, respectively"]. More alarming, the first nine months of FY2026 saw $3.6 billion in inventory provisions—over four times the $876 million in the prior-year period [SOURCE: filings/NVDA/10-Q__2025-11-19__0001045810-25-000230.htm | "$3.6 billion and $876 million for the first nine months of fiscal years 2026 and 2025, respectively"].

This spike in provisions suggests either overproduction, demand miscalculation, or accelerated product transitions—all of which threaten gross margins. Inventory provisions are expensed in cost of revenue, directly reducing profitability. A fourfold increase in such charges over nine months indicates deteriorating inventory efficiency, even as revenue grows.

Further compounding risk is a sharp rise in capital commitments. Property, equipment, and intangible assets acquired but not yet paid for totaled $790 million in the first nine months of FY2026, compared to "not significant" in the same period of FY2025 [SOURCE: filings/NVDA/10-Q__2025-11-19__0001045810-25-000230.htm | "Property, equipment and intangible assets acquired but not paid for the first nine months of fiscal years 2026 and 2025 were $ 790 million and not significant, respectively"]. This $790 million in unpaid obligations represents a material increase in near-term cash outflow risk, potentially constraining financial flexibility.

While NVDA continues aggressive capital returns—repurchasing $9.7 billion of stock in FY2026Q2 alone and maintaining a $14.7 billion buyback authorization as of July 27, 2025 [SOURCE: filings/NVDA/10-Q__2025-08-27__0001045810-25-000209.htm | "As of July 27, 2025, we were authorized, subject to certain specifications, to repurchase up to $ 14.7 billion of our common stock"]—this is being funded in part by strong cash generation, but increasingly may rely on cash reserves or debt. The combination of rising inventory provisions and unpaid capital commitments suggests a shift toward capital inefficiency.

Cross-source evidence confirms the disconnect between fundamentals and valuation. Earnings beats (structured facts) drove sustained price appreciation (price data), while regulatory filings reveal growing cost pressures (10-Q). The narrative context—NVDA named in a "Mag 7" article about laggards turning into winners—suggests market sentiment remains optimistic [SOURCE: news/NVDA/a01422e8948989c4a7355620adfaca0504c9b45df6e74fe250235007aba0929e.json | "How the Mag 7’s 2025 Laggards Could Turn Into 2026 Winners"]. But NVDA was not a laggard: its 34.8% return outpaced most peers. The label reflects narrative overreach, not reality.

**Falsification criteria**: This thesis would be invalidated if NVDA’s inventory provisions in FY2026Q4 fall below $200 million *and* unpaid property/equipment commitments decline sequentially, while revenue growth remains above 20% YoY. Such a result would confirm improved inventory control and capital efficiency, justifying the current premium.

Absent such a turnaround, the stock’s rise reflects sentiment and momentum, not sustainable fundamentals. The evidence shows NVDA is executing strongly but at rising cost. The market has priced in flawless continuation. It has not priced in reversal risk. That makes the stock **vulnerable to a correction on any sign of demand softness or margin compression**.

**Stance: NVDA is priced for perfection — and is therefore overvalued relative to its escalating operational risks.**
