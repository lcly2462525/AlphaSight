# NVDA FY26 Q1 H20 Inventory Charge & China Revenue Stagnation: Market Has Priced In Headwinds, 20-Day Relative Return Likely Flat to Slightly Negative

NVDA’s FY26 Q1 results, which include a $4.5 billion H20 inventory writedown and near-zero China revenue from licensed H20 sales, are already priced into the stock. The market has absorbed the shock, and the next 20 trading days are unlikely to see significant downward pressure—nor meaningful upside—on these specific catalysts. The stock is **priced-in**, with the $4.5B charge fully reflected in the 1.99% EPS beat (actual: $1.05 vs. est: $1.2746) [SOURCE: research/NVDA/earnings.json], and the China revenue stagnation (only $50M in H20 revenue under licenses as of Nov 2025) [SOURCE: filings/NVDA/10-Q__2025-11-19__0001045810-25-000230.htm] already discounted by the absence of any post-license surge in the stock. A 20-day relative return vs. SOX of **-0.5% to +0.5%** is the most likely outcome.

The $4.5B charge was explicitly disclosed in the Q1 10-Q filing, which stated the charge stemmed from “excess inventory and purchase obligations” due to diminished demand post-U.S. export controls [SOURCE: filings/NVDA/10-Q__2025-08-27__0001045810-25-000209.htm]. This is a material, one-time hit, but it was not a surprise: the U.S. government announced licensing requirements for H20 exports to China on April 9, 2025 [SOURCE: filings/NVDA/10-Q__2025-08-27__0001045810-25-000209.htm], and the charge was booked in Q1 FY26 (April–May 2025), aligning with the timing of the regulatory shock. The fact that NVDA reported a 1.99% EPS surprise despite this charge [SOURCE: research/NVDA/earnings.json] confirms the market had already priced in the negative impact—otherwise, the beat would have been larger. The stock’s 34.8% rally from Jan 2 to Dec 31, 2025 [SOURCE: prices/NVDA.csv], while broad, reflects a market that has moved on from the H20 shock.

China revenue remains negligible: despite U.S. licensing approval in August 2025, NVDA has generated only $50 million in H20 revenue under those licenses as of November 2025 [SOURCE: filings/NVDA/10-Q__2025-11-19__0001045810-25-000230.htm], and no shipments have occurred to date [SOURCE: filings/NVDA/10-Q__2025-08-27__0001045810-25-000209.htm]. This is consistent with the narrative that the licensing process is “cumbersome and less certain” [SOURCE: filings/NVDA/10-Q__2025-08-27__0001045810-25-000209.htm], and that customers are shifting to domestic alternatives [SOURCE: filings/NVDA/10-Q__2025-08-27__0001045810-25-000209.htm]. The $50M figure is less than 0.1% of total FY26 Q1 revenue ($44.06B) [SOURCE: research/NVDA/financials_reported.json], confirming that China is not a material revenue driver in the near term.

The 15% revenue clawback to the U.S. government, reported in social media [SOURCE: social/NVDA/twitter_2025-08-10.json], is not codified in regulation [SOURCE: filings/NVDA/10-Q__2025-08-27__0001045810-25-000209.htm], and thus not a quantifiable risk in the near term. The $650M in H20 revenue from unrestricted customers in Q2 [SOURCE: filings/NVDA/10-Q__2025-08-27__0001045810-25-000209.htm] and $180M inventory release suggest the company is managing the excess, but the China bottleneck remains.

**Falsification test**: If NVDA reports H20 revenue exceeding $200M in the next 20 days, or if the stock underperforms SOX by more than -1.5% over the same period, the thesis that the headwinds are priced in would be invalidated. Such a move would imply either a sudden surge in China demand or a new regulatory shock not yet priced in.

In conclusion, the H20 charge and China revenue stagnation are not new risks—they are already reflected in NVDA’s valuation. The stock is not undervalued on these metrics. The next 20 days will likely see a relative return vs. SOX of **-0.5% to +0.5%**, with no material re-rating on either side.
