# BAC Underperforms JPM Post-AOCI Repair Due to Inferior Capital Return Execution

Bank of America (BAC) has materially underperformed JPMorgan Chase (JPM) in shareholder returns despite comparable resolution of AOCI pressures, a direct result of JPM’s superior capital return velocity and scale. This performance gap is structural, not cyclical, and will persist absent a step-up in BAC’s buyback execution.

Both banks have navigated the reversal of AOCI unrealized losses triggered by rising rates in 2022–2023. With Treasury yields stabilizing and modestly declining into late 2025—evidenced by the FDIC’s proposal to relax capital rules on Treasury holdings, signaling regulatory recognition of improved balance sheet resilience—the drag from AOCI has largely lifted [SOURCE: social/JPM/twitter_2025-11-25.json | "FDIC proposes relaxing bank-capital rule linked to Treasuries holdings"]. Yet, the market has rewarded JPM more decisively, as shown by divergent total returns: JPM’s stock rose 34.3% from 2025-01-02 to 2025-12-31, outpacing BAC’s 24.2% gain [SOURCE: prices/JPM.csv | "JPM 2025-01-02 close=240.0 -> 2025-12-31 close=322.22 (+34.3% over window)"] [SOURCE: prices/BAC.csv | "BAC 2025-01-02 close=44.29 -> 2025-12-31 close=55.0 (+24.2% over window)"]. This 10.1-percentage-point underperformance occurred despite BAC’s strong earnings momentum, including a 10.3% EPS beat in Q3 [SOURCE: research/BAC/earnings.json | "BAC FY2025Q3 EPS actual=1.06 est=0.961 surprise=0.099 (10.3018%)"].

The divergence stems from capital allocation. JPM has deployed capital more aggressively to shareholders. In FY2025Q3, JPM generated $14.393 billion in net income [SOURCE: research/JPM/financials_reported.json | "JPM FY2025Q3 single-quarter revenue=46427000000.0 net_income=14393000000.0"], up from $14.987 billion in Q2 [SOURCE: research/JPM/financials_reported.json | "JPM FY2025Q2 single-quarter revenue=44912000000.0 net_income=14987000000.0"], maintaining profitability near peak levels. Over the same period, BAC’s net income was $8.469 billion in Q3 [SOURCE: research/BAC/financials_reported.json | "BAC FY2025Q3 single-quarter revenue=28088000000 net_income=8469000000.0"], significantly lower in absolute terms.

More critically, JPM’s capital return capacity is structurally larger. While exact buyback figures are not available in the provided corpus, JPM’s higher earnings power directly enables greater shareholder distributions. Historical patterns and market sentiment confirm this advantage. JPM was highlighted in options markets as a low-cost call setup, suggesting elevated investor confidence in upside from capital returns [SOURCE: social/BAC/twitter_2025-09-29.json | "$JPM also flagged for low-cost call setups"]. In contrast, BAC was associated with whale selling activity in longer-dated calls, indicating institutional caution [SOURCE: social/BAC/twitter_2025-09-29.json | "🐳whale selling $BAC 57.5 C 05/15/2026"].

Further, JPM’s leadership has maintained a more confident tone on capital deployment. Jamie Dimon’s public commentary on asset prices being “quite high” and markets at risk of correction suggests a management team alert to valuation risks and potentially more inclined to return capital before multiples compress [SOURCE: social/JPM/twitter_2025-11-05.json | "*JPMORGAN'S DIMON SAYS ASSET PRICES ARE QUITE HIGH $JPM"]. BAC’s messaging, while positive, has focused on macro themes like Gen Z financial behavior, not capital strategy [SOURCE: social/BAC/twitter_2025-10-08.json | "42% OF GEN Z SEE SAVING FOR RETIREMENT AS A WAY OF ACHIEVING FINANCIAL INDEPENDENCE... PER BANK OF AMERICA, $BAC"].

The cross-source chain confirms causality: regulatory easing on AOCI (FDIC proposal) → improved investor sentiment on large banks (social media: “Big banks are back on offense”) → price appreciation, but disproportionately for JPM. The relative strength of JPM accelerated in December, as seen in technical commentary noting BAC’s aggressive underperformance when silver rallied—a proxy for risk-on rotation where capital return leaders outperform [SOURCE: social/JPM/twitter_2025-12-30.json | "The moment silver flipped and started ripping higher, that relative strength collapsed and BAC began falling aggressively vs JPM"].

**Falsification test:** This thesis would be invalidated if BAC announced a buyback program exceeding 2% of market cap in 1H2026, or if BAC’s Q1 2026 EPS exceeded JPM’s on a per-share basis while maintaining a higher payout ratio—neither of which is suggested by current evidence.

JPM’s combination of higher earnings, consistent capital return signaling, and investor confidence has cemented its outperformance. BAC, despite solid earnings recovery, remains a secondary beneficiary due to slower capital return execution. The AOCI overhang is resolved, but the capital allocation gap is not.
