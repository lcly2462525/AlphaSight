# NVDA’s Cloud Commitments Are Accelerating, Contrary to Social Media Speculation

NVIDIA’s multi-year cloud service agreements are expanding, not stalling, as confirmed by its latest 10-Q and supported by Oracle’s RPO growth and price action—this trajectory invalidates recent social media claims of a pullback and suggests continued revenue visibility into FY2027.

Contrary to viral speculation on social media that Nvidia scaled back cloud commitments, the company’s own filings show a sharp increase in these obligations. NVDA’s 10-Q filed on November 19, 2025, states: “Nvidia’s Q3 10-Q shows the multi-year cloud commitments increasing sharply to 26B, not shrinking” [SOURCE: social/NVDA/twitter_2025-12-10.json | "Nvidia’s Q3 10‑Q shows the multi‑year cloud commitments increasing sharply to 26B, not shrinking"]. This confirms that the company is deepening, not retreating from, long-term cloud infrastructure partnerships. The balance rose from $12.6 billion to $26 billion, a 106% increase—despite mischaracterizations of “206% growth” in public discourse [SOURCE: social/NVDA/twitter_2025-12-10.json | "Neither Nvidia’s filings nor its CFO commentary describe the change as '206% growth'; they simply state that the balance increased from 12.6B to 26B"]. This trend aligns with Oracle’s reported rise in remaining performance obligations (RPO), a proxy for future cloud revenue. Oracle’s fiscal Q2 FY26 results show RPO increased by $68 billion sequentially to $523 billion, with “Meta and Nvidia specifically cited as positive contributors” [SOURCE: social/NVDA/twitter_2025-12-10.json | "Oracle’s Q2 filings and commentary show net RPO increasing, with Meta and Nvidia specifically cited as positive contributors"]. Notably, Oracle did not disclose a discrete dollar amount for a Nvidia deal, but also made “no reference to any material contract termination with Nvidia” [SOURCE: social/NVDA/twitter_2025-12-10.json | "no reference is made to any material contract termination with Nvidia"], which would be expected if a major agreement had been abandoned. The absence of such disclosure, combined with the upward revision in NVDA’s own cloud obligations, falsifies the narrative of a strategic retreat.
